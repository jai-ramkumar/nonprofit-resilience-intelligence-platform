## Paper 1

**Title:** Resiliency Tactics During Financial Crisis: The Nonprofit Resiliency Framework
**Authors:** Elizabeth A. M. Searing, Kimberly K. Wiley, Sarah L. Young
**Journal:** Nonprofit Management and Leadership, Volume 32, Issue 2, Pages 179-196
**Year:** 2021
**DOI:** 10.1002/nml.21478

### Key Finding

Most nonprofit research focuses on predicting organizational closure rather than understanding how nonprofits survive and continue delivering services during crises. This paper fills that gap by studying 31 nonprofits during the Illinois state budget impasse (2015-2017) and identifying the Nonprofit Resiliency Framework across five tactical areas: financial, human resources, outreach, programs and services, and management and leadership.

### Notes Relevant to This Project

- The paper argues that financial vulnerability studies are limited because they focus on closure, not recovery. This directly supports the motivation of this research project, which focuses on early warning and decision support rather than predicting failure.
- Financial tactics identified include cashflow monitoring, tapping lines of credit, developing new funding sources, reducing ancillary costs, revenue portfolio diversification, and asset liquidation. These align with the indicators proposed in the Financial Resilience Score.
- The paper confirms that revenue concentration (heavy reliance on government funding) is a significant source of organizational risk. This supports the Government Funding Dependency indicator in Phase 1.
- The authors note that accounting ratios are "blunt instruments" for describing organizational resilience and call for more nuanced, prescriptive frameworks. This supports the decision-support framing of this project over a purely predictive one.
- IRS Form 990 data was used alongside interviews to assess revenue stability and cash flow during the crisis. This confirms Form 990 as a recognized and accepted data source in the nonprofit resilience literature.

### One Key Quote to Remember

The authors define resiliency as "the ability to withstand adverse conditions while still delivering services." This is broader than financial survival and connects directly to the Capacity Gap Framework concept of comparing community need growth against organizational capacity.

## Paper 2

**Title:** True Grit: Exploring Nonprofit Sector Resilience Following Economic Recessions
**Authors:** Xintong Chen, Kerry Kuenzi, Lindsey Evans, Amanda Stewart
**Journal:** Nonprofit Policy Forum, Volume 16, Issue 4, Pages 573-601
**Year:** 2025
**DOI:** 10.1515/npf-2023-0058

### Key Finding

Using a fixed effects panel regression model covering 29 years of IRS Form 990 data (1989 to 2018) and three economic recessions, the study finds that organizational size, age, and revenue structure are the most consistent predictors of nonprofit sector resilience. Larger and older organizations recover more reliably after recessions. Contribution revenue and earned income both positively predict revenue resilience.

### Notes Relevant to This Project

- The study uses NCCS Core Public Charities files, which are compiled from IRS Form 990 data. This reinforces the validity of using public 990 data as the primary data source for quantitative nonprofit resilience research.
- Financial reserves (measured as equity ratio) did not significantly predict resilience in most recession periods. The authors note this may be because the equity ratio cannot distinguish between restricted and unrestricted funds. This is a useful data limitation to acknowledge in the methodology, as the ProPublica API will have similar constraints.
- Revenue diversification findings are nuanced. Contribution revenue and earned income both support resilience, but over-reliance on either single source can be a risk. This supports the revenue concentration indicators in the Financial Resilience Score.
- The study finds that community factors, specifically the evenness of resource distribution across nonprofits in a community, affect resilience in some recession periods. This is relevant to the long-term Capacity Gap Index design, which plans to incorporate community-level indicators in Phase 2.
- The paper distinguishes between total expense resilience and total revenue resilience as separate dimensions of organizational recovery. This distinction is useful when designing the Financial Resilience Score to ensure it captures both resource attraction and mission expenditure.

### One Key Finding to Remember

Organizational size consistently predicts resilience across all three recession periods. This suggests that size should be treated as a control variable or segmentation factor when applying the Financial Resilience Score across organizations of different sizes in Phase 1.

| Theme | Searing et al. 2021 | Chen et al. 2025 |
|---|---|---|
